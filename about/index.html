The Science-Backed Journey of Julio Mendez

Hello, and welcome.

My name is Julio Mendez. I was born in Central America in 1982 and moved to Las Vegas, Nevada, in 1984 during a record-setting summer that I still hear stories about today. Las Vegas has been home for most of my life.

I graduated from Vo-Tech High School in Las Vegas in 2000 and have spent much of my adult life pursuing education in health, science, and physical conditioning. Over the years, I have earned various credentials and licenses, including personal training and CNA certifications, while continually expanding my knowledge of health and human performance.

Formal education is the core foundation of my training methodologies. My protocols are built directly upon the biological laws of cell adaptation and chemical metabolic pathways.

My formal education took me to Southern Utah University, where I earned two distinct Bachelor of Science degrees. The first was in Biology with an emphasis in Physiology, focusing primarily on Comparative Physiology. The second was in Chemistry with a Health Emphasis, which included extensive academic areas such as Medicinal Chemistry and health-related sciences.

After graduation, I attended graduate school in Iowa, reaching my second year of study before life presented challenges that made it difficult to continue. Although I did not complete the degree, the rigorous graduate environment taught me critical lessons about perseverance, research data analysis, and personal adaptation.

Ironically, despite my deep educational background in the physical sciences, my own serious transformation journey did not begin until 2018. At that time, I reached approximately 270 pounds. I was highly uncomfortable with how I looked and felt, and I had become so self-conscious that I avoided having my picture taken for several years.

Rather than looking for shortcuts or unscientific fast-tracks, I started with biological basics. My workouts consisted primarily of low-stress aerobic conditioning. During my first year, I gradually worked up to 45 minutes of cardio six days per week while performing only 10 to 20 minutes of targeted resistance training four days per week.

The physiological adjustments were immense. By the end of that first year, I had reduced my total mass to approximately 188 pounds. Once I returned to Las Vegas, I decided to pursue a new competitive challenge: bodybuilding.

As I began increasing my training intensity to stimulate safe muscular development, my weight climbed back to a healthier 205 pounds. During that time, an experienced bodybuilding mentor noticed my metrics and offered to coach me. Under his direct guidance, I eventually reduced my weight to approximately 174 pounds while reaching nearly 6% body fat.

The transformation was profound, but more importantly, I discovered an enduring passion for bodybuilding and biomechanics that continues to drive me. Even in my 40s, I am actively building muscle, refining my physique, and working toward new competitive benchmarks.

One of the biggest lessons I have learned is that intellectual knowledge and physical application must operate together. Despite my academic background, there were times early on when I trained harder than I trained smarter. When physical progress comes quickly, it is tempting to ignore joint recovery, overlook metabolic warning signs, and assume more volume is always better.

It is not.

Through both competitive success and joint injuries, I have learned the importance of balancing hard training with strategic recovery, cellular nutrition, and long-term joint integrity.

This website is where I publish those exact structural lessons. My ultimate goal is to provide highly practical, science-backed resources that help athletes protect their joints, optimize their biology, and build physical capability regardless of age or starting point.
